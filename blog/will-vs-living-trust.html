Will vs. living trust: which one do you need?

By John McManus March 2025

When it comes to passing assets to your heirs, most people know they need either a will or a trust — but the differences between the two often aren't clear until you're deep in the planning process. Here's a plain-language comparison.

What a will does

A will is a legal document that states how you want your assets distributed after death, and who should be the guardian of any minor children. It only takes effect when you die, and it must go through probate — the court-supervised process for validating the will and overseeing distribution.

What a living trust does

A revocable living trust holds your assets during your lifetime and passes them to beneficiaries after death — without probate. You act as your own trustee while you're alive and name a successor to take over when you pass or become incapacitated.

Side-by-side comparison

  • Probate: Will goes through probate; trust does not
  • Privacy: Will becomes public record; trust stays private
  • Incapacity planning: Will does nothing during your lifetime; trust allows seamless transition
  • Cost to set up: Will is generally cheaper upfront; trust costs more to establish but saves significantly on probate fees
  • Complexity: Will is simpler; trust requires transferring asset titles into the trust

Can you have both?

Yes — and many estate plans include both. A "pour-over will" is often used alongside a trust to catch any assets that weren't transferred into the trust before death, directing them into it posthumously (though they'd still go through probate).

The right choice depends on the size and complexity of your estate, your family situation, and your priorities. John McManus is happy to talk through what makes sense for you.
