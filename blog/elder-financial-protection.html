Elder Care

Protecting aging parents from financial exploitation

By John McManus December 2024

Elder financial abuse is one of the most prevalent — and underreported — forms of elder abuse in the United States. According to various studies, it affects millions of older adults each year and costs billions of dollars. And despite the image of the anonymous scammer, the perpetrator is often a family member, caregiver, or trusted acquaintance.

What does financial exploitation look like?

It can range from outright theft or forgery to subtler forms of manipulation — pressuring an elderly person to change their will, "borrowing" money without repaying it, or taking over finances under the guise of helping. Cognitive decline makes older adults particularly vulnerable, as does social isolation.

Warning signs to watch for

  • Unusual bank withdrawals or transfers, especially to unfamiliar accounts
  • Unpaid bills despite adequate income or savings
  • A new person suddenly becoming very involved in financial decisions
  • Changes to a will, trust, or beneficiary designations that the person can't explain
  • The older adult seems confused, fearful, or reluctant to discuss money

Protective steps you can take now

The best protection is planning before a crisis occurs:

  1. Get legal documents in place. A durable financial POA with a trusted agent — or a professional fiduciary — limits the window of vulnerability.
  2. Set up account monitoring. Many banks offer alerts for large transactions or allow a trusted contact to be notified of unusual activity.
  3. Limit access. Not everyone who offers to help needs full access to finances. A fiduciary provides accountability that a family member managing finances informally doesn't.
  4. Talk about it openly. Families that discuss financial plans openly make it harder for bad actors to operate in secret.
